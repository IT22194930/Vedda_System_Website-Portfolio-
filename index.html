🔬 Research Domain

Research Domain

Understanding the challenges, gaps, and innovative approaches to preserving endangered indigenous heritage.

00 - Literature Survey

Literature Survey

Prior research shows that cascaded translation pipelines are effective for multilingual systems, and Sinhala-English neural machine translation can provide a practical bridge for related low-resource languages such as Vedda [1], [2]. Studies on phonemic transcription also emphasize that languages without standardized writing systems need both manual and automated annotation support to make speech data usable at scale [3].

Work on Vedda preservation consistently points to documentation, digitization, and technology-enabled support as the core requirements for survival [4], [5]. Web platforms have improved access to indigenous language resources, but many still focus on content delivery rather than learner assistance [6]. In contrast, AI-based vocabulary learning research highlights the value of adaptive support and guided practice, which this project extends to Vedda language learning [7].

For digital heritage preservation, OAIS provides a robust archival model while CIDOC CRM supports interoperable cultural metadata and semantic description [8], [9]. Automated identification research further shows that deep learning and hybrid CNN-SVM methods can perform strongly on structured classification problems, making them suitable for artifact recognition workflows [10].

Speech visualization studies demonstrate that 3D articulatory feedback improves pronunciation learning by exposing mouth and tongue motion instead of relying only on text or audio [11]. Recent lip-sync and tongue-animation methods confirm that realistic motion improves intelligibility and naturalness [12], [13], and WebGL talking-head systems show that these ideas can run directly in browsers [14].

Research implication: the proposed platform combines browser-based 3D rendering, IPA-driven viseme control, translation support, archival metadata, and adaptive learner guidance to address the current gap in Vedda-centered intelligent support.

01 - Problem

Research Problem

The Vedda language of Sri Lanka is critically endangered - an oral-only language spoken by fewer than 300 people, with no written script, formal grammar, or digital footprint. As elder speakers pass away, entire vocabularies, oral histories, and cultural knowledge are lost irreversibly.

Without immediate intervention through modern digital preservation technologies, this unique linguistic and cultural identity faces complete extinction within a single generation.

02 - Research Gap

Research Gap

Despite significant advances in natural language processing and digital heritage technologies, a critical and underserved gap exists for extremely low-resource, oral-only indigenous languages like Vedda. Current preservation and educational solutions fall across four interconnected dimensions:

01. Culturally-Contextualized Translation Pipelines

Existing neural machine translation models require massive datasets and frequently hallucinate or fragment culturally significant compound phrases when applied to endangered languages. No current system utilizes a progressive algorithm and intermediate bridge-language architecture to ensure semantic and cultural fidelity without massive data overhead.

02. Intelligent gamified adaptive learning systems for Data-Scarce Languages

Current language learning platforms rely on rigid rule-based systems or generic large language models that invent invalid vocabulary (hallucinations) when lacking domain knowledge. There is an absence of user engaging adaptive learning systems that provide accurate, culturally grounded pedagogical feedback.

03. Low-Resource Phonetic and Speech Preservation

As an oral-only language without a standard script, Vedda cannot be preserved through text alone. Existing speech technologies fail on micro-corpora, and there is a lack of integrated platforms with interactive, 3D phonetic visualization tools to accurately capture, transcribe, and teach indigenous pronunciation.

04. AI-Assisted Cultural Heritage Documentation

Traditional digital archives for indigenous cultures are static, text-heavy, and lack automated metadata generation. No integrated platform currently employs hybrid machine learning pipelines (such as CNN-SVM architectures) optimized for extremely limited datasets to accurately identify and digitally catalog traditional artifacts and cultural metadata.

03 - Research Objectives

Research Objectives

To design and develop a comprehensive digital preservation and learning platform that safeguards the highly endangered Vedda language and culture. This system aims to support multilingual communication, interactive education, and cultural documentation through advanced artificial intelligence and scalable microservices.

01. Real-Time Translation and Speech Processing

Develop a bidirectional translation and speech transcription service tailored for low-resource constraints. This objective focuses on implementing a Sinhala-bridge architecture with progressive phrase matching and a fine-tuned Whisper model for accurate Vedda speech recognition.

02. AI-Driven Adaptive Learning and Feedback

Design an intelligent learning module that provides gamified vocabulary practice and adaptive guidance. This involves utilizing a hybrid ensemble machine learning classifier for real-time mistake detection and a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) pipeline to deliver personalized, culturally accurate pedagogical feedback.

03. Interactive 3D Phonetic Visualization

Create an interactive 3D pronunciation model to preserve and teach the oral traditions of the Vedda language. This objective centers on mapping International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA) data to viseme-based morph targets, enabling realistic 3D facial animations for accurate pronunciation guidance.

04. AI-Assisted Cultural Artifact Identification

Develop a culturally governed digital preservation system to automate the metadata identification of traditional Vedda artifacts. This objective employs a hybrid Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) and Support Vector Machine (SVM) pipeline to ensure high-accuracy artifact classification under data-scarce conditions.

04 - Methodology

Methodology

The Vedda Language Preservation Platform was developed following an Agile methodology with iterative development sprints, enabling continuous validation of research hypotheses, machine learning model refinement, and rapid adaptation based on field data and expert linguistic feedback.

PHASE 1 - Research & Requirements

Conducted a comprehensive literature survey on low-resource language preservation and digital heritage. Formulated the research problem, defined measurable objectives for the four core subsystems, and finalized the microservices system architecture design. Technology selection was completed (incorporating Python, Node.js, React, and Three.js) and the project proposal was drafted and submitted.

PHASE 2 - System Foundation

Configured the Docker bridge network (vedda-network) to containerize independent microservices. Set up the central API Gateway using Flask for routing, Cross-Origin Resource Sharing (CORS), and JSON Web Token (JWT) validation. Established database schemas across MongoDB and SQLite, and scaffolded the React and Three.js frontend environments.

PHASE 3 - Core Module Development

Parallel development of all four core modules: the Sinhala-bridge translation and Whisper-based Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) pipeline, the AI-driven adaptive learning module featuring a Hybrid Ensemble classifier and GPT-4 Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) integration, the cultural artifact identifier using a CNN-SVM hybrid model, and the interactive 3D phonetic viewer with IPA-driven lip-synchronization.

PHASE 4 - Integration & Validation

Full cross-module system integration via REST APIs and shared volumes. Conducted rigorous performance validation of ML models against baselines (e.g., 5-fold cross-validation and Word Error Rate benchmarking). Executed real-world data collection and validation through site visits to Vedda communities (Dambana), followed by final latency optimization for production readiness.

Technologies Used

Technologies Used

These are the technologies used across the project modules for real-time translation, adaptive vocabulary learning, 3D phonetic pronunciation, and artifact digital preservation.

  • Web Technologies React, Vite, Node.js, and Flask for building the interactive client frontend, backend microservices, and the centralized API Gateway.
  • Databases & Storage MongoDB, SQLite, and Cloudinary for managing artifact metadata, user learning progress, system history, and media content delivery.
  • AI / ML Frameworks Scikit-learn and Keras/TensorFlow for developing the hybrid CNN-SVM image classification pipeline and the soft-voting machine learning ensemble (Logistic Regression, Random Forest, Gradient Boosting).
  • Speech and Translation Fine-tuned Whisper ASR, gTTS (Google Text-to-Speech), and Google APIs for multilingual speech-to-text processing and Sinhala-bridge text translation.
  • Adaptive Learning & GenAI OpenAI API (GPT-4 and Embeddings) integrated with a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) pipeline to deliver context-aware, personalized pedagogical feedback.
  • 3D and Media Three.js, WebGL, and GLTF/GLB components for rendering 3D facial models with IPA-driven morph target mapping for interactive lip-synchronization.
  • Infrastructure & Security Docker (Bridge Network) for containerized microservice orchestration, alongside JSON Web Tokens (JWT) and bcrypt for secure routing and authentication.
  • Deployment - GitHub, Vercel, and Azure for source control and hosting

System Architecture

A modular microservices layout connecting the client, gateway, AI services, data stores, and external APIs into one preservation platform.

Project Proposal & Viva

📅 Report Deadline: 15 August 2025 ⏱ Presentation: 8 – 12 September 2025 📊 Total Weight: 12%

The Project Proposal is the first formal milestone, establishing the academic and commercial foundation of the research. The assessment opens with a two-minute promotional video that introduces the real-world problem and its impact, accompanied by a high-level system diagram illustrating the proposed solution's architecture and component relationships.

Each student is then allocated exactly three minutes to present their individual component, focusing specifically on the knowledge gap they are addressing, the specialized technologies selected, and a clear, structured implementation roadmap. The session concludes with a group analysis of the project's commercialization potential, covering estimated investment costs, pricing strategies, and target market profiles for the proposed solution.

Progress Presentation 1 (PP1)

📅 Date: 5 – 9 January 2026 ⏱ Duration: 20 Minutes per Team 📊 Total Weight: 15%

Progress Presentation 1 is a critical individual milestone that serves as an academic reality check, verifying that each student is on track to effectively solve their stated research problem while exposing any design gaps, technical inconsistencies, or unanticipated implementation challenges early enough in the development cycle to allow meaningful course correction.

Each student must demonstrate that at least 50% of their technical product is functionally complete, presented through a live demo, simulation, or working software prototype. The 20-minute team session is scored across three components: individual presentation quality, the depth and clarity of the technical demonstration, and performance in a viva Q&A session with the panel. For mobile applications, demonstrations must be conducted via emulator, physical devices are not permitted.

The prototype may range from an initial alpha to a pre-alpha release, provided it runs successfully and showcases meaningful system features with realistic data rather than basic interface elements such as login screens.

Progress Presentation 2 (PP2)

📅 Date: 9 – 12 March 2026 ⏱ Duration: 45 Minutes per Team 📊 Total Weight: 18%

Progress Presentation 2 is a comprehensive 45-minute team assessment evaluating the depth of system integration, research maturity, and the team's ability to demonstrate a near-complete, cohesive product. A randomly selected team member opens with a five-minute project overview before the group delivers a 30-minute integrated live demonstration spanning all four system modules working together seamlessly.

At this stage, the system must be at least 90% complete with full cross-module integration clearly demonstrated. Research maturity is assessed through data-backed performance claims, baseline comparisons against existing approaches, and evidence of meaningful experimental validation with real or representative data. The scoring framework is weighted heavily toward Solution Implementation (40%) and Applied Knowledge (20%), alongside communication quality and the team's articulation of the project's commercialization potential.

Teams must ensure they bring all required hardware including laptops, HDMI cables, and extension cords and maintain a pre-recorded backup video as a contingency plan for technical failures during the live demonstration.

Final Assessment

📅 Date: 27th April – 6th May 2026 📊 Total Weight: 55%

The Final Assessment is the most comprehensive evaluation of the project, representing 55% of the overall module grade. It is the culmination of an entire year of research, development, and iterative refinement and at this stage, examiners expect a fully completed, production-ready system that satisfies every stated research objective with no missing requirements, unresolved defects, or incomplete features.

The evaluation panel will rigorously examine each student's understanding of the AI and machine learning components embedded within the system including how models were designed, trained, validated, and integrated into the production application. Students must be prepared to defend their architectural decisions, explain their code at a granular level, discuss edge cases and system limitations, and demonstrate awareness of scalability, deployment considerations, and real-world operational constraints.

Beyond the live system demonstration, the final assessment encompasses a comprehensive set of written and digital deliverables including individual and group reports, a research paper, a project logbook, this website, and checklist documentation each contributing to the holistic evaluation of the team's research output and professional standard.
